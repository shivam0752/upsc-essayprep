    UPSC Essay 2025 — Section A
    Question 1

    Truth knows no color.

    Bhakti Movement Epistemology Socio-Cultural

    ◆ The Shroud of Kabir

    In the humid, dust-choked lanes of 15th-century Varanasi, the Bhakti mystic Kabir composed verses that cut clean through the sectarian divide of medieval India. To the Hindu orthodoxy, truth was colored in the saffron of rituals, caste hierarchies, and Sanskrit scriptures. To the Islamic clergy, truth was wrapped in the green of formal dogmas, Arabic texts, and institutionalized power. Kabir rejected both, declaring that the ultimate reality was an unpainted, formless presence—*Nirguna*.

    Upon Kabir's death in Maghar, a fierce dispute erupted. The Hindus demanded his body for cremation, claiming him as their guru; the Muslims insisted on burial, claiming him as their Sufi Pir. As the confrontation grew violent, someone drew back the white shroud covering his body. To their absolute bewilderment, the corpse had vanished. In its place lay a heap of fresh, fragrant flowers.

    Dumbfounded, the two groups divided the flowers. The Hindus cremated their share; the Muslims buried theirs. The colors of their rites remained different, but the entity they sought to possess had transitioned beyond both, proving that Truth belongs to no single faction.

    "Just as the flowers beneath Kabir's shroud dissolved the structural boundary between cremation and burial, objective truth remains independent of the ideological prism—be it political, cultural, or religious—through which humanity attempts to paint it."

    Conclude the essay by recalling the Maghar sanctuary, reminding the examiner that as we struggle with polarized echo chambers, our global salvation lies in seeking the "flowers under the shroud"—the objective, uncolored truths of human dignity and ecological survival.

    Question 2

    The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting.

    Indian History Statecraft International Relations

    ◆ Ashoka's Transition to Dhamma-Vijaya

    In 261 BCE, the plains of Kalinga ran red with the blood of over a hundred thousand slain warriors. Standing amidst the carnage, the Mauryan Emperor Ashoka felt the hollow weight of *Sastra-vijaya*—victory achieved by the sword. He realized that while military might could annex territory, it left the minds of the survivors hostile and unyielding. The real enemy was not the Kalingan army, but the resentment and chaos of subjugation.

    Rather than deploying legions to suppress subsequent rebellions, Ashoka initiated an unprecedented structural pivot in statecraft. He formulated *Dhamma-vijaya* (conquest through righteousness). Instead of generals, he dispatched *Dhamma-mahamattas* (emissaries of peace), and sent his own children, Mahindra and Sanghamitra, to Sri Lanka armed not with bows, but with a sapling of the sacred Bodhi tree.

    Without marching a single battalion, Ashoka secured the spiritual and cultural allegiance of kingdoms across East and Southeast Asia. His influence outlasted the Mauryan empire by millennia, demonstrating Sun Tzu's timeless axiom that true victory lies in aligning minds, not breaking bodies.

    "Ashoka's transition from the bloodied fields of Kalinga to the moral diplomacy of Dhamma reveals that lasting security is never built upon physical subjugation, but on the systematic cultivation of shared values and mutual trust."

    Return to the contrast between Ashoka's sword and his stone edicts. Argue that in a thermonuclear age, where physical combat means mutual assured destruction, modern superpowers must master this supreme art by investing in soft power, multilateralism, and global ethical frameworks.

    Question 3

    Thought finds a world and creates one also.

    Idealism Science History Cognitive Science

    ◆ Ramanujan's Intuitive Mock Theta Functions

    In the early 20th century, Srinivasa Ramanujan sat on the porch of his humble home in Kumbakonam, Tamil Nadu, armed only with a slate and red chalk. He lived in a world where formal mathematical proofs were the currency of credibility, a world he had not been formally trained to navigate. Yet, Ramanujan’s mind bypassed conventional logic. He claimed that the goddess Namagiri of Namakkal wrote complex mathematical equations on his tongue in dreams.

    Ramanujan did not just discover existing mathematical relations; his thoughts built entirely new paradigms. On his deathbed in 1920, with a failing body, he penned a letter to G.H. Hardy detailing what he called "Mock Theta Functions"—abstract mathematical constructs that did not fit into any known framework of his time.

    For nearly a century, these equations remained a mystery. It was only in the 2000s that physicists realized Ramanujan's mock theta functions are essential to calculating the entropy of black holes—cosmic phenomena that Ramanujan could not have observed. His mind found the abstract laws of numbers and, in doing so, created the framework to decode the deepest mysteries of the physical universe.

    "Ramanujan's scribbles on a simple slate demonstrate that human cognition is not a passive mirror reflecting the physical world, but an active forge that structures and materializes the reality of tomorrow."

    Bring the reader back to the image of Ramanujan's chalk dust on his slate. Suggest that our current challenges—climate change, AI governance—are waiting for shifts in human imagination to create new, sustainable socio-economic worlds.

    Question 4

    Best lessons are learnt through bitter experiences.

    World History Existentialism Resilience

    ◆ The Crucible of Gitanjali

    Between 1902 and 1907, Rabindranath Tagore was pushed into a dark crucible of grief. In rapid succession, he lost his wife, Mrinalini Devi, his daughter, Renuka, and his youngest son, Shamindranath, who died of cholera at a tender age. The profound loneliness and raw pain of these bereavement years threatened to break his creative spirit.

    Instead of retreating into bitterness, Tagore channeled his grief into poetry. The songs of *Gitanjali* (Song Offerings) were born during this dark period. He transformed his deep personal sorrow into a universal quest for spiritual connection, translating the isolation of death into a deeper communion with the divine and the beauty of life.

    In 1913, *Gitanjali* won the Nobel Prize in Literature. It was not a product of intellectual comfort, but a wisdom refined by suffering. Tagore's bitterest losses taught him a profound empathy that resonated across the globe, illustrating that the human spirit learns its deepest truths not in the sunshine of prosperity, but in the shadows of adversity.

    "Tagore's transformation of intense bereavement into the transcendent verses of Gitanjali reveals that suffering, when processed through creative and reflective resilience, becomes the primary catalyst for civilizational and personal growth."

    Conclude by referencing Tagore's song of the soul rising above physical decay. Remind the reader that nations, like individuals, must use historical tragedies (such as colonialism or pandemics) as lessons to build robust social and institutional safety nets.

    UPSC Essay 2025 — Section B
    Question 5

    Muddy water is best cleared by leaving it alone.

    Eastern Philosophy Administrative History Governance

    ◆ Lao Tzu and the Principle of Wu-Wei

    In ancient China, during the chaotic Warring States period, the philosopher Lao Tzu observed rulers attempting to fix social decay through aggressive regulation, heavy taxes, and constant military interventions. He likened their hyperactive governance to a person trying to clear a muddy pool by stirring it with a stick—an action that only suspended the sediment further.

    Lao Tzu introduced the concept of *Wu-Wei* (effortless action or non-interference). To demonstrate this to his disciples, he told a story of a traveler who came across a stream churned up by a passing horse carriage. The disciple eagerly offered to step in and filter out the mud. Lao Tzu stopped him and said, "Wait." They sat in silence. Slowly, gravity pulled the mud down. The leaves settled. Within an hour, the water was crystal clear and fit to drink.

    In public policy, this is often seen in the contrast between over-regulation and letting organic markets or communities self-regulate. When the state over-intervenes in complex systems, it creates noise; sometimes, the wisest policy is to create the space for natural equilibrium to restore itself.

    "Lao Tzu’s muddy stream is a powerful warning against the modern administrative instinct of hyper-regulation, showing that in complex socio-economic systems, patient containment often succeeds where hasty intervention fails."

    Recall the image of the settling stream in the final paragraph. Emphasize that in times of crisis, leaders must balance active intervention with the calm patience of Wu-Wei, letting the sediment of panic settle before charting a long-term course.

    Question 6

    The years teach much which the days never know.

    Historiography World History Strategic Foresight

    ◆ Ibn Khaldun's Contemplative Isolation

    In the 14th century, the scholar and diplomat Ibn Khaldun was caught in the volatile politics of North Africa. He watched dynasties rise, betrayals unfold, and kingdoms collapse in a matter of days. To someone living through those daily headlines, history seemed like a chaotic series of accidents, driven by the whims of individual rulers.

    Exiled and seeking refuge in the fortress of Qalat Ibn Salama in modern-day Algeria, Khaldun spent five years away from the active courts. Looking back over the decades of his life and centuries of Islamic history, he realized that the daily news was a distraction from long-term trends. He wrote the *Muqaddimah* (Introduction to History), where he identified the concept of *Asabiyyah*—the cyclical nature of social cohesion that determines the rise and fall of empires over generations.

    While the "days" of his political career were filled with temporary crises, the "years" of structured reflection revealed the hidden laws of sociology. This story highlights the importance of historical perspective over short-term reactionary thinking.

    "Ibn Khaldun's formulation of the cyclical laws of history shows that while daily events capture our immediate attention, it is only through long-term structural analysis that we can understand civilizational changes."

    Conclude by warning against the "tyranny of the immediate" in our modern digital landscape. Re-invoke Ibn Khaldun's silent fortress to argue that true statesmanship requires looking past daily media cycles to plan for the decades ahead.

    Question 7

    It is best to see life as a journey, not as a destination.

    Indian History Buddhist Philosophy Ethics

    ◆ Xuanzang's Quest for the Scriptures

    In 629 CE, the Chinese monk Xuanzang secretly left Chang'an, defying an imperial travel ban. His goal was Nalanda in India, where he hoped to acquire and translate original Sanskrit scriptures to resolve contradictions in Chinese Buddhist texts. To an outside observer, his destination was the library of Nalanda; his success would be measured solely by the cargo of scrolls he brought back.

    However, Xuanzang's journey became a transformative saga. For 17 years, he navigated the shifting sands of the Gobi Desert, scaled the frozen peaks of the Tian Shan, and survived capture by river bandits. Along the way, he learned local dialects, debated scholars, mediated disputes between kings, and developed a profound cross-cultural empathy.

    When he finally returned to China with hundreds of manuscripts, the Emperor offered him high political office. Xuanzang declined. He spent his remaining days in a modest pagoda translating the texts. The destination (Nalanda) was merely the canvas; it was the arduous journey that transformed him into an ambassador of cultural synthesis.

    "Xuanzang's historic journey demonstrates that the value of human ambition lies not in the final acquisition of a goal, but in the cognitive and ethical development forged along the way."

    Link Xuanzang's physical journey back to the contemporary pursuit of national development. Suggest that India's journey toward progress must prioritize democratic and ethical values over a narrow focus on GDP targets.

    Question 8

    Contentment is natural wealth; luxury is artificial poverty.

    Classical Philosophy Ancient History Ecological Ethics

    ◆ Diogenes and the Emperor's Sunlight

    In the 4th century BCE, Alexander the Great, the ruler of Macedon, was at the height of his power. Having conquered Greece and prepared to march into Asia, he possessed wealth, military power, and imperial prestige. Yet, his ambitions left him restless; he was trapped in a cycle of wanting more territory—a state of artificial poverty where no amount of acquisition was enough.

    Curious to meet the philosopher Diogenes of Sinope, Alexander went to the Corinthian suburbs. He found Diogenes sunbathing near a wooden tub, which served as his only shelter. Diogenes possessed nothing but a cloak, a staff, and a wooden cup (which he threw away after seeing a child drink from their hands).

    Alexander, standing over him, asked, "Is there any favor I can grant you?" Diogenes replied, "Yes. Stand out of my sunlight." Alexander was struck by this response. While the emperor was burdened by the demands of his empire, the philosopher was content with the light of the sun. Alexander reportedly remarked to his officers, "If I were not Alexander, I would wish to be Diogenes."

    "The exchange between Alexander and Diogenes highlights the contrast between the insatiable greed of artificial poverty and the freedom of self-determined contentment."

    Conclude the essay by contrasting modern consumerism with Diogenes' sunlight. Argue that to address climate change and resource depletion, we must shift our metrics of success from material consumption to sustainable contentment.

    UPSC Essay 2024 — Section A
    Question 1

    Forests precede civilizations and deserts follow them.

    Environmental History Ecological Ethics Socio-Economic

    ◆ The Ecological Salinization of Sumer

    Around 3000 BCE, the ancient Sumerians established the cradle of human civilization in the fertile floodplains between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers. Initially, the region was surrounded by wetlands and upstream forests in the Zagros mountains, which regulated the water flow and stabilized the soil. Civilization flourished, giving rise to writing, mathematics, and complex urban planning.

    To support their growing cities, the Sumerians cleared the upstream forests for timber and created massive canal networks to irrigate the arid lowlands. However, they lacked an understanding of drainage. The intensive irrigation in a hot climate raised the water table, bringing dissolved salts to the surface.

    Over centuries, the fertile soil became encrusted with salt, transforming it into a sterile desert. Wheat yields plummeted, leading to famine, political fragmentation, and the eventual collapse of the Sumerian kingdoms. What began as a land of agricultural abundance ended as a barren desert, illustrating the consequences of ignoring ecological limits.

    "The salinization of Sumer shows that when a civilization views nature merely as a resource to be exploited, it sets in motion the process of its own ecological collapse."

    Remind the reader that modern cities face similar risks. Re-invoke the salt-encrusted ruins of Sumer to urge a shift toward ecological restoration, green infrastructure, and sustainable agricultural practices.

    Question 2

    The empires of the future will be the empires of the mind.

    Information Age Knowledge History Technology

    ◆ The Survival of the House of Wisdom

    In 1258 CE, the Mongol forces led by Hulagu Khan ransacked Baghdad, then the intellectual capital of the world. They targeted the *Bayt al-Hikmah* (House of Wisdom), throwing hundreds of thousands of scientific, philosophical, and mathematical manuscripts into the Tigris River. It was said the river ran black with the ink of books and red with the blood of scholars. The physical empire of the Abbasids was completely destroyed.

    However, the intellectual legacy survived. Scholars like Nasir al-Din al-Tusi had smuggled out key manuscripts before the siege, preserving them at the Maragheh Observatory. Over the next century, the Mongol conquerors themselves began to adopt the culture, science, and religion of the people they had defeated, eventually converting to Islam and patronizing learning.

    The physical conquest was temporary, but the intellectual empire of Baghdad outlived the Mongol military empire. In the modern era, this pattern is reinforced: true power is increasingly driven by knowledge, software, and cultural influence rather than physical territory.

    "The persistence of the House of Wisdom shows that physical conquest is temporary; lasting influence belongs to the ideas, innovations, and values that shape the human intellect."

    Conclude by arguing that India’s future strength lies not in territorial disputes, but in developing its human resources, fostering scientific research, and leveraging its democratic ideals as a source of global soft power.

    Question 3

    There is no path to happiness; Happiness is the path.

    Zen Buddhism Bhakti Philosophy Ethics & Well-being

    ◆ Shantideva's Silent Practice

    In the 8th century, at the Buddhist university of Nalanda, a monk named Shantideva was regarded by his peers as a failure. They nicknamed him *Bhusuku*—one who only eats (*bhu*), sleeps (*su*), and walks (*ku*). The other monks, believing him to be lazy and unlearned, decided to humiliate him by demanding he deliver a public sermon to the entire assembly.

    Shantideva ascended the teaching throne and asked if they wanted to hear something old or something new. They requested something new. He then recited the *Bodhicaryavatara* (The Way of the Bodhisattva), a text on compassion and the nature of the mind. He explained that peace and joy are not rewards at the end of a long journey, but qualities to be cultivated in each moment of practice.

    As he reached the verses on the ultimate nature of reality, it is said Shantideva levitated into the air and vanished from sight, though his voice continued to echo through the hall. The monks realized that his seemingly quiet life was not a sign of laziness, but a continuous practice of mindfulness. He had integrated peace into his daily activities, showing that happiness is the method, not the destination.

    "Shantideva’s sermon reminds us that when we treat peace and contentment as goals to be achieved after reaching certain milestones, we miss the opportunity to practice them in the present."

    Encourage the reader to step off the treadmill of constant acquisition. Re-invoke Shantideva's sermon to argue for a lifestyle that prioritizes present-moment awareness, ethical clarity, and gratitude.

    Question 4

    The doubter is a true man of science.

    Scientific Method Islamic Golden Age Epistemology

    ◆ Alhazen and the Optics of Doubt

    In 11th-century Cairo, the scholar Hasan Ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen) was placed under house arrest by the Caliph Al-Hakim after a project to regulate the Nile failed. Confined to a dark room for ten years, Alhazen turned his attention to the study of light. At the time, scientific thought was dominated by Ptolemy and Euclid's "emission theory," which claimed that the eyes project rays of light to perceive objects.

    Rather than accepting this established authority, Alhazen questioned it. He conducted systematic experiments using a darkened room with a small hole in one wall—a *camera obscura*—to demonstrate that light travels in straight lines from objects into the eye.

    His willingness to doubt accepted theories laid the foundation for the empirical scientific method. He wrote: "The seeker after the truth is not one who studies the writings of the ancients and, following his natural disposition, puts his trust in them, but rather the one who suspects his faith in them and questions his own understanding."

    "Alhazen’s breakthrough in optics shows that scientific progress is driven not by the collection of facts, but by the willingness to question and test established assumptions."

    Conclude by emphasizing the importance of skepticism and critical thinking in an era of digital misinformation. Re-invoke Alhazen's dark room to argue that a healthy democracy requires citizens who ask for evidence rather than accepting dogmas.

    UPSC Essay 2024 — Section B
    Question 5

    Social media is triggering 'Fear of Missing Out' amongst the youth, precipitating depression and loneliness.

    Existential Philosophy Mental Health Technology Ethics

    ◆ Schopenhauer's Porcupine Dilemma

    In his 1851 work *Parerga and Paralipomena*, the philosopher Arthur Schopenhauer presented a parable about a group of porcupines on a cold winter day. To protect themselves from the freezing cold, they huddled close together for warmth. However, as they drew nearer, their sharp quills began to prick one another, causing pain that forced them to separate.

    Cold and shivering, they huddled together again, only to experience the same discomfort. They repeated this cycle until they found a moderate distance that allowed them to stay warm without pricking each other.

    In the digital age, social media has disrupted this delicate balance. In our loneliness, we seek connection online, but the curated lifestyles we see can trigger comparison, anxiety, and a fear of missing out. The digital "huddle" can leave users feeling more isolated, highlighting the challenge of building meaningful connections online.

    "Schopenhauer’s porcupine dilemma highlights the challenge of modern hyper-connectivity: in our rush to avoid loneliness through digital platforms, we often expose ourselves to the anxiety of comparison."

    Suggest that the solution lies in finding a healthy digital distance. Re-invoke the porcupine metaphor to advocate for digital literacy, screen-time boundaries, and the cultivation of authentic, face-to-face community relationships.

    Question 6

    Nearly all men can stand adversity, but to test the character, give him power.

    Ancient History Political Theory Ethics & Leadership

    ◆ Cincinnatus and the Return to the Plow

    In 458 BCE, the Roman Republic faced an invasion by the Aequi and Sabines, threatening the city with defeat. In panic, the Senate turned to Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus, a respected former consul who had retired to work on his small farm along the Tiber River. The Senate appointed him Dictator, granting him absolute military and political authority over Rome.

    Cincinnatus left his plow, assumed command, mobilized the army, and defeated the invading forces in a campaign lasting fifteen days. With the crisis resolved and the city safe, Cincinnatus possessed the authority to remain dictator for six months.

    Instead, he immediately resigned his commission, gave up absolute power, and returned to his farm to resume plowing his fields. By choosing his farm over the opportunity to rule, Cincinnatus demonstrated that true character is revealed by how one handles the acquisition of power.

    "Cincinnatus’s resignation of absolute power shows that the true test of leadership lies not in the ability to acquire authority during a crisis, but in the restraint to step down when the task is done."

    Contrast Cincinnatus with modern leaders who seek to extend their terms. Re-invoke the image of his abandoned plow to advocate for institutional checks, constitutional morality, and the value of selfless public service.

    Question 7

    All ideas having large consequences are always simple.

    Indian History Political Economy Social Movements

    ◆ Gandhi and the Pinch of Salt

    In early 1930, as the Indian National Congress sought a way to challenge British colonial rule, Mahatma Gandhi proposed a march to the sea to protest the salt tax. Many political colleagues were skeptical, questioning how a basic commodity like salt could serve as the focal point for a national independence movement.

    Gandhi recognized that salt was a daily necessity for every Indian, regardless of religion, caste, or class. On March 12, 1930, he walked 240 miles from Sabarmati Ashram to the coast of Dandi. On April 6, he bent down and picked up a handful of natural salt from the shore, violating the colonial monopoly.

    This simple gesture resonated across the country, inspiring millions of citizens to manufacture salt and boycott British goods. The protest challenged the moral authority of the colonial administration, showing how a simple idea can mobilize a nation.

    "Gandhi’s pick of salt shows that the most effective political ideas are often simple, relying on universal human needs to unite diverse populations."

    Use the Dandi March to remind the reader that addressing complex modern issues requires finding simple, relatable core values—like fairness, clean air, and community dignity—to build consensus.

    Question 8

    The cost of being wrong is less than the cost of doing nothing.

    Cold War History Crisis Management Administrative Decision-Making

    ◆ The Decision of Stanislav Petrov

    On September 26, 1983, Lt. Col. Stanislav Petrov was the officer on duty at the Serpukhov-15 bunker near Moscow, which monitored the Soviet Union's early-warning satellites. Shortly after midnight, the bunker's alarms sounded, indicating that the United States had launched five Minuteman intercontinental ballistic missiles.

    Soviet military protocol required Petrov to report the warning immediately to his superiors, which would likely have triggered a retaliatory nuclear strike. However, Petrov questioned the data, noting that the system was new and that a real first strike would involve hundreds of missiles, not just five.

    Petrov decided to treat the alarm as a system malfunction, choosing to wait for confirmation rather than initiating a response. If he was wrong, the Soviet Union faced destruction; if he did nothing to retaliate, he risked failing his duty. His decision to pause and verify proved correct, as the warning was later identified as a solar reflection off high-altitude clouds, preventing a potential nuclear conflict.

    "Petrov's decision shows that in high-stakes situations, a thoughtful pause and willingness to verify information can be more effective than executing protocol without question."

    Conclude by arguing that modern administration faces similar challenges. Re-invoke Petrov's decision to show that leadership requires the judgment to evaluate information under pressure rather than relying solely on automated systems.
